Robot Kinematics & Dynamics: Basic Definitions and Design

To build a robot arm that works in real life, we must first understand how it moves and how its parts work together. This starts with learning about its structure and motion — known as kinematics and dynamics.

Basic Parts of a Robot Arm

  • Links: These are solid parts that form the body of the robot. Each link connects to the next through a joint.
  • Joints: Joints allow movement between links. They can rotate or slide.
  • Actuators: Motors or devices that move the joints.
  • Transmissions: Systems like gears or pulleys that pass motion from actuators to joints.
  • Sensors & Controllers: Sensors collect info like position or force. Controllers use this to move the robot correctly.
  • User Interface: Allows a person to control or monitor the robot.

Designing the Mechanism

Once we know the task and space the robot will work in, we design the arm's shape. There is no single perfect design. It depends on:

  • How far and how flexible the robot must reach
  • How heavy the objects are
  • How fast and precise it must be
  • Cost and ease of building it

Example: If the robot must cut plastic sheets, we design the arm based on the space available, cutting speed, and plastic type.

Kinematic Analysis

Kinematics is about motion — not forces. It includes:

  • Position: Where the robot's tool is and how it is oriented
  • Velocity: How fast the joints or end are moving
  • Acceleration: How quickly the speed changes

Dynamic Analysis

Dynamics deals with forces and torques. It helps us answer:

  • How much torque is needed at each joint?
  • What motors are suitable?
  • How much power is required?

These answers are key to choosing the right motors and ensuring the robot works safely and effectively.
